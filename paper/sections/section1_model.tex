\section{Primal Structure and Benchmark}
\label{sec:model}

This paper studies a signaling environment with two sender types and two signals, where the same signal that can separate today also creates a near-term reputation risk that may be repriced tomorrow. The baseline is the dynamic extension of Spence logic needed for the fixed-point reversal.

\subsection{Types, signals, and judgment states}

There is a sender with type
\[
\theta\in\{\theta_L,\theta_H\},\qquad \theta_H>\theta_L.
\]
The receiver does not observe the type.

The sender chooses one of two signals
\[
s\in\{s_A,s_P\}.
\]
Signal $s_A$ is the ambitious action, and $s_P$ is the pooling-safe action.

Judgment intensity follows a public Markov state
\[
j_t\in\{L,H\}.
\]
State $L$ is ordinary evaluation, and state $H$ is high-scrutiny evaluation. Let
\[
\mu_H>\mu_L>0
\]
denote the scrutiny multipliers and
\[
q:=\Pr(j_{t+1}=H\mid j_t=L)
\]
be the transition probability from low to high. The sender observes $q$ before choosing a signal.

\subsection{One-period exposure shadow}

Choosing $s_A$ creates a one-period exposure state that can be repriced tomorrow. Let
\[
X_t\in\{0,1\}
\]
indicate whether last period's action is exposed in the next period. If $s_t=s_A$, then $X_{t+1}=1$; if $s_t=s_P$, then $X_{t+1}=0$. In this baseline the exposure horizon is one period: choosing the safe signal today removes the next-period exposure created by a yesterday ambitious action.

If $X_{t+1}=1$ and $j_{t+1}=H$, the sender incurs a future epistemic penalty proportional to a true-type exposure index. This is the anticipatory shadow that drives behavior in the low state.

\subsection{Current payoff and the anticipatory rent}

Let $B_\theta$ denote the current net separation surplus from $s_A$ versus $s_P$ in state $L$ when the receiver interprets the ambitious signal in the high-type way. The low-state payoff gain from sending $s_A$ is
\[
D_\theta^L(q)=B_\theta-\beta q\mu_H\Phi_\theta,
\]
where $\Phi_\theta>0$ is the true-type exposure cost derived from primitive self-model accuracy (Section~\ref{sec:surprise}), and $\beta\in(0,1)$ is the discount factor.

The type-$\theta$ IC threshold is
\[
q_\theta^*:=\frac{B_\theta}{\beta\mu_H\Phi_\theta},
\]
whenever $B_\theta>0$. Type $\theta$ is willing to send $s_A$ in the low-scrutiny state iff
\[
q<q_\theta^*.
\]

\subsection{Static benchmark}

When $q=0$, current risk from future repricing is absent. The model then collapses to a one-shot separating benchmark.

\begin{assumption}[Static separation]
\label{ass:static-separation}
\[
B_H>0,\qquad B_L<0.
\]
\end{assumption}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:static-separation}, with no anticipated repricing $s_A$ is uniquely attractive only to the high type.

\subsection{Anticipatory reversal object}

The model's distinctive case is high-type exposure vulnerability: the same primitive that makes ambitious separation valuable can make the high type more exposed to adverse repricing. Assumption~\ref{ass:static-separation} therefore identifies the low-scrutiny rent before future exposure is priced; it does not guarantee that the high type keeps using \(s_A\) when the transition probability into high scrutiny is large. Section~\ref{sec:belief-loop} defines the IC and D1 thresholds and shows how their maximum selects safe pooling.
